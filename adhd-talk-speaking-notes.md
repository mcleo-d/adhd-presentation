# Late Diagnosis — Speaking Notes

**Men's Health session · ~27 min talk + 15 min Q&A · 7 slides**

How to use this: bullets are cues, say them in your own words. Lines in **bold quotes** are the few worth saying close to verbatim. `[PAUSE]` means stop and let it land — count three. Don't read this off the screen; glance, then talk to the room.

## SLIDE 0 — Title / Montage · ~2 min

*(Montage rolling. Talk over it before you introduce yourself.)*

- **"Every photo you're looking at has something in common. It was there in all of them, shaping me, and I had no idea. That thing is ADHD."**
- These pictures span my whole life. Birth, to now, to 50.
- Quick, honest housekeeping — say it light: **"Before I go further — I'm not a doctor. Nothing here is medical advice. This is just my story."**
- The promise: **"By the end I'll have joined the dots on these photos. And some of you might recognise your own life in them."**
- *Then:* "I'm James."

`[PAUSE]` — let the montage finish before the first content slide.

---

## SLIDE 1 — Two versions of me · ~4 min

- For most of my life there were two versions of me running at once.
- **What people saw:** capable, gets there in the end, calm enough.
- **What it actually took:** constant overdrive, effort I hid, a quiet kind of tired that never really lifted.
- The gap between those two is where the imposter feeling lived.
- **"I always felt one step out of sync with the room."** Like everyone got a manual I never received.
- I didn't have a word for it. So I assumed it was me. A flaw. Something to push through.

`[PAUSE]`

## SLIDE 2 — The wall and the mirror · ~5 min

*(This is the heart. Slow down. Don't rush to the diagnosis.)*

- Two things happened at almost the same time.
- **"I burned out."** Be honest but measured — name it, don't dwell in it. One line on what that felt like.
- **"And my son was diagnosed with ADHD."**
- Watching his assessment, hearing them describe him... I was hearing my own life read back to me.
- **"I saw myself in him."**

`[PAUSE]` — this is your biggest pause of the talk.

- Bridge line, said into the silence — **no slide change:**
- **"And it made me ask a question I couldn't un-ask. What if it had been there the whole time?"**

---

## SLIDE 3 — A different operating system (+ the clip) · ~6 min

- When you're diagnosed late, you never fully stop asking: is this real, or am I making it up?
- I want to play you something. This was the moment I stopped asking.
- Set it up honestly: **"It's not all me — the bit about marriage isn't my story. But the part about how we think? That's me to a tee."**

`[PAUSE]` — let the clip's last line hang for a second.

- Pick it up: that's been my whole career. Built around things I'm genuinely interested in. Solving problems in ways that don't always look conventional.
- The honest flip side: **"Seeing things differently is one thing. Getting other people to see what I see — that's the real work. I'm still learning to bridge that gap."**
- The reframe — say it plainly, this is the turn:
- **"For 49 years I thought I was lazy, or broken, or just... different. I wasn't any of those. I was running a different operating system. Once I understood that, everything else started to make sense."**

## SLIDE 4 — What changed · ~7 min

*(Longest slide. Three legs: understanding, strategies, support. Watch your clock here above all — if you overrun, trim the hyperfocus detail.)*

**Understanding (the biggest one)**
- The relief wasn't "now I'm fixed." It was: it had a name, and it was never a character flaw.

**The work story — the concrete bit**
- Before, in meetings: technically demanding, context switching fast, and I was brand new to the place.
- **"I couldn't follow. I couldn't show people who I actually was. And I knew something was wrong — I just thought it was my age. It wasn't. It was ADHD I'd carried my whole life."**
- The everyday cost — say this slowly, it'll land: **"I was avoiding taking anything on. Staying on top of what was already in front of me was enough. The thought of one more task — however small — felt overbearing."**

`[PAUSE]`

**Strategies & support — including medication**
- Frame it as new and unfinished: **"I was formally diagnosed on the 29th of August 2025. So I'm about nine months in, still being titrated, still figuring this out."**
- What it is, plainly: Elvanse is a stimulant medication for ADHD, prescribed and monitored by a clinician.
- The monitoring detail — good modelling for this room: **"Because it's a stimulant, I check my blood pressure and heart rate regularly. That's part of managing it properly."** *(Frame intervals as what YOUR clinician set, not a general rule.)*
- The difference — avoid "miracle": **"The difference was night and day. For the first time, the noise went quiet and I could just... think."**

**Hyperfocus — both sides, no incident**
- **"One thing I've felt kick in is hyperfocus. I'll lock into a meeting I'm enjoying and the questions and ideas just keep coming — it's the version of me that was locked away for years, finally in the room."**
- The flip side: it can be a lot, and the right level is still being tuned. That's the honest reality of being early in this. *(If asked in Q&A: leading ADHD researchers push back on calling this a superpower — because you can't control when it switches on or where it points.)*

**The turn outward — the climax**
- **"There's a phrase I really dislike: 'ADHD is a superpower.' It sounds positive, but it does damage. It hides how hard the daily management is, and it piles on pressure to keep over-delivering. I'm not Superman."**
- The ask of the room: **"People with ADHD shouldn't just be 'the alternative view' round the table — wheeled out for a fresh angle, then talked over. Understanding ADHD is a business responsibility, not just a personal one. How you speak to us, how you run a meeting — it decides whether we contribute, or spend the whole time masking. And that costs the business the very thinking it says it values."**

## Delivery reminders (not for slides)

- Remote room of 50: build in deliberate pauses; you can't read faces, so silence is your friend.
- Clip is your biggest tech risk — cue to 1:30, share audio, dry-run with a colleague, know your stop point.
- Fallback if audio fails: read the key line as a quote — *"so many of us are pattern recognition people... once you find your true self you stop putting effort into the wrong places and realise what you're actually interested in."*
- Trim point if overrunning: the hyperfocus detail on Slide 4.
- Make the ask of the business (Slide 4) BEFORE you go tender (Slide 5). Order matters.
